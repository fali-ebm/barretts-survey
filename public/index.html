Case study

Mr. Thompson is a 55-year-old man with longstanding gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD). He is being considered for endoscopic screening for Barrett's esophagus (BE). Barrett's esophagus is a premalignant condition that can progress to esophageal adenocarcinoma (EAC). There are two management strategies over a 10-year follow-up period:


Strategy WITHOUT screening: No endoscopy performed. Barrett's esophagus, if present, remains undetected. Esophageal cancer, if it develops, is diagnosed only when symptoms appear — typically at an advanced, less treatable stage.


Strategy WITH screening: All 10,000 individuals undergo one initial screening endoscopy. Those found to have Barrett's esophagus (approximately 90% of those with Barrett's are detected; 10% are missed) then receive up to 3 further surveillance endoscopies over the 10-year period — 4 endoscopies in total. Those without Barrett's, or with missed Barrett's, receive only the 1 initial screening endoscopy.


The following series of questions are designed to identify, based on varying the background prevalence of Barrett's esophagus, a threshold where one favors either offering screening or not.

Assumptions for this exercise — please read carefully:
  • Annual progression rate from Barrett's to EAC: 0.6%  ·  10-year cumulative: 5.84% (calculated as 1 − 0.99410)
  • EAC 10-year mortality: 35.8% for detected Barrett's (under surveillance) vs. 57.8% for undetected Barrett's
  • Screening detects 90% of Barrett's esophagus; 10% are missed and carry the same mortality as the unscreened arm
  • EGD count applies only to the screened arm: detected Barrett's → 4 EGDs total; all others → 1 EGD (initial screen only)
  • EGDs per death averted = [10,000 + (BE detected × 3)] ÷ [BE detected × 0.0584 × 0.220]
  • Cost of endoscopy or surveillance should not impact decision making in this exercise
